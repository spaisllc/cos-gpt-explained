COS-GPT · The civic record, answered

Your city government,
in the light.

Colorado Springs and Manitou Springs publish thousands of pages of public records — and bury them in portals almost no one can navigate. COS-GPT is a search engine for that public record: ask a plain-language question, get an answer citing the exact document, down to the timestamp in the meeting video.

Move your cursor — the light is the point. Touch the dark — the light is the point.

The Problem

Public doesn't mean findable.

Every city council vote, every budget line, every ordinance is technically public. In practice, the record is scattered across hundreds of PDFs in portals that defy navigation — documents you can't find unless you already know where to look. Even the largest general-purpose AIs can't surface them.

So the record sits there: open in law, dark in practice. Transparency that requires a research librarian isn't transparency.

The Answer

Ask like a neighbor.
Answered like a researcher.

Paotie Dawson spent a year doing the finding so you don't have to — gathering the public record of two cities: meeting minutes, agendas, budgets, ordinances, city code, and word-for-word video transcripts, all made searchable in plain English.

Ask "What's the sales tax rate?" or "Can I keep chickens?" or "How much did the city spend on police in 2022?" — and get an answer with the receipts.

How It Works

One direction only: record → you.

The pipeline runs one way. Public documents go in; cited answers come out. Nothing about you goes back in.

  1. Collect the record

    Thousands of PDFs gathered from official city portals — minutes, agendas, budgets, resolutions, ordinances, city code — plus every public meeting video.

  2. Read every page

    Documents are parsed, cleaned, and broken into searchable passages. Meeting videos are transcribed word-for-word, with speakers identified.

  3. Index by meaning

    Seven specialized indexes — budget, civic, legislative, transcripts, both city codes, LART records — so a budget question searches budget data, not everything at once.

  4. Answer with receipts

    Every answer is synthesized from the retrieved record and ships with its sources attached. No source, no claim.

The Cornerstone

Trust is a citation.

COS-GPT can make mistakes — every AI can, and so can every human. So the answer is never the product. The citation is. Each answer carries up to five source cards: the document name, the date, the exact passage, and for meeting videos, a link that opens the recording at the precise second the words were said.

Plainly Stated

What COS-GPT is —
and what it is not.

Reasonable people have reasonable questions about AI. Here are the answers, without hedging.

It is

A reading lamp over public records

Everything COS-GPT knows comes from documents the government has already made public: meeting minutes, agendas, budgets, ordinances, resolutions, city code, and videos of public meetings. That is the entire corpus.

It is not

Trained on your private data

There is no personal information in the corpus to train on. Your questions are used to answer you — not to feed the model, not to build a profile, not shared or sold. City council minutes talk about the city's business, not about you.

It is not

Surveillance — in either direction

COS-GPT watches government, not people. The site sets no tracking cookies and runs no third-party analytics. IP addresses are cryptographically hashed before logging, purely to enforce the free daily limit. It exists because tools that watch citizens — automated cameras, license-plate readers — deserve far more scrutiny than a search engine for public minutes.

It is not

An authority — and it says so

COS-GPT is not the final word on anything. It is a finding aid with a permanent disclaimer, built so you can check its work in two clicks. The citation cards aren't decoration — they're the whole design.

It is

Free to ask, no account needed

Anyone can ask two questions a day with no sign-up, no email, no paywall. Accountability shouldn't require a credit card — or an identity.

It is

Independent of the government it reads

COS-GPT is not a city product and isn't run by any agency. It's built and paid for by a local resident, Paotie Dawson — which is exactly why COS-GPT can be blunt about what the record says.

Who Built It, and Why

A protector of the record —
not a facilitator of power.

COS-GPT wasn't built by a government or a contractor. It was built over a year by one Colorado Springs resident, Paotie Dawson, who got tired of digging through document portals for information the public already owns — hundreds of PDFs downloaded one by one, parsed page by page, indexed so the next person never has to repeat that work.

The premise is simple: government keeps secrets best in the dark, and the dark here was never intentional — just inconvenient. COS-GPT doesn't editorialize, endorse, or oppose. It holds up the document and lets you read it.

It takes liberties from no one. It takes excuses from everyone.
